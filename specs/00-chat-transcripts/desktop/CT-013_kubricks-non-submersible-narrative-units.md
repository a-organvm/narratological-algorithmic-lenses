In the context of Stanley Kubrick's filmmaking philosophy, "Non-Submersible Units"refers to his theory of narrative structure. Kubrick famously stated that a film should not be a continuous, fluid narrative, but rather a collection of 6 to 8 distinct, self-contained sequences. He called these sequences "non-submersible" because each one is built to function independently with its own beginning, middle, and end—like solid blocks that stand on their own without needing the surrounding plot to prop them up.
 
The Theory Explained
 
Kubrick believed that traditional narrative—where scene A leads inevitably to scene B via cause-and-effect—was less important than creating intense, memorable experiences. By structuring a film as a series of "units," he forced the audience to do the intellectual work of connecting them, resulting in a more subjective and profound engagement with the themes. 
* The Structure: 6 to 8 distinct sequences per film.
* The Goal: To strip away non-essential exposition and focus on high-impact visual storytelling.
* The Origin: Kubrick discussed this theory with writer Brian Aldiss (author of Supertoys Last All Summer Long) and it is documented in the series Stanley and Us.
Case Study: 2001: A Space Odyssey
2001: A Space Odyssey is the purest application of this theory. The film does not flow like a standard story; it jumps across millions of years between massive, isolated blocks of time that are thematically linked but narratively disconnected. 
1
Unit 1
The Dawn of Man: A completely silent, self-contained nature documentary about hominids discovering tools via the Monolith.
2
Unit 2
The Lunar Journey: Millions of years later, Dr. Heywood Floyd travels to the moon. This unit introduces the sci-fi setting and ends with the discovery of the second Monolith (TMA-1).
3
Unit 3
The Jupiter Mission: The film shifts genres into a survival thriller. This unit focuses on the battle between Bowman, Poole, and the computer HAL 9000.
4
Unit 4
Jupiter & Beyond the Infinite: The final psychedelic sequence abandons dialogue entirely, moving into pure visual metaphor as Bowman transforms into the Star Child.
Other Examples
* Full Metal Jacket: The film is famously split into two starkly different non-submersible units: the training camp (Unit 1) and the Vietnam war (Unit 2). They feel like two different movies stitched together.
* The Shining: Kubrick organized the film using title cards ("The Interview," "Closing Day," "A Month Later") to create rigid temporal blocks that isolate the Torrance family's descent into madness. 
Common Confusion: The AE-35 Unit: While the term "Non-Submersible Unit" refers to narrative structure, 2001: A Space Odyssey also features a famous plot device called the AE-35 Unit. This is the piece of antenna hardware that HAL 9000 falsely predicts will fail.
