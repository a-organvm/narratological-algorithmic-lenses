## PART II: SYNOPSIS OF EVERY DRAFT

### D0–D1: "A Girl and Her Dogs" (October–November 2017)

**Original Conception**

El (14), a mute Latina foster child, lives with adoptive parents Sandra and Dave in a Florida apartment complex. The screenplay opens with a FLASH-FORWARD to Sheen's death—establishing the central mystery from the first frames.

**Structure**:
- Multiple death scenarios for Sheen presented in montage (yellow corvette trunk, suicide staging, foaming drink)
- El witnesses police arriving at Sheen's apartment
- Narrative then rewinds to El's ordinary world: morning routines, blog posts, Dave's unemployment announcement

**Key Elements**:
- El's muteness established but not explained
- Sandra and Dave's bickering characterizes the household
- Sheen (23) introduced as chaotic neighbor with yellow race-car boyfriend Frank
- Dog-walking emerges as El's entrepreneurial solution to family financial strain
- Supporting cast: Hugh (elderly Jewish neighbor), Gary (pit-bull owning landlord), Jeannie (cigarette-smoking neighbor), Russian brothers managing the apartments

**Tonal Register**: More grounded realism with surrealist punctuation; the vines imagery present but less developed.

---

### D2: "Tale of El" (December 2017)

**Title Change Significance**: The shift from "A Girl and Her Dogs" to "Tale of El" signals a move from external descriptor (the dogs) to internal focus (El herself). The word "Tale" suggests folkloric or mythic framing.

**Structural Refinements**:
- Tightened opening sequence
- Expanded Sheen relationship
- More developed school sequences with Mia and Emily (bullies)
- Courtroom fantasy sequence more elaborated

**Key Addition**: The courtroom sequence, originally hinted at, becomes a fuller set piece with El's internal resistance ("*No.*") as the climactic beat.

---

### D3: "Tale of El" (January 2018)

**Consolidation Draft**

This version integrates feedback from Aaron and Adam. The screenplay settles into its three-act structure:

**Act I**: Ordinary world disruption (Dave fired, El needs job, dog-walking solution)
**Act II**: Sheen friendship deepens, investigation after Sheen's death, encounters with suspects
**Act III**: Courtroom fantasy/reality sequence, final confrontation with Sandra

**Ending (D3)**:
> "They tell El she must get rid of Albie, and with her voice she says, 'no.'"

This is the version referenced in the Treatment—explicit vocalization of "no."

### D7: Seventh Draft (August 2019)

**Final Submitted Version**

This is the draft submitted to the Nicholl Fellowship competition.

**Revolutionary Structural Addition: The Body Metaphor**

D7 opens with an entirely new framing device:

```
FADE IN: a translucent BODY drops a glowing yellow orb into the top of its viscous head.

The orb spirals down, dissolving in The Body's gut then branching through The Body's veins.
```

"The Body" functions as:
1. Dissociative representation of El's self-perception
2. Structural throughline (present at opening, resolved at ending)
3. Psychological realism marker (trauma manifests as body alienation)

**Key Changes from D4**:

| Element | D4 | D7 |
|---------|----|----|
| El's age | 14 | **16** |
| Race-car color | Yellow | **RED** |
| Landlord/Antagonist | Julian (male) | **Julia (female)** |
| Officer/Judge | Daryl (male) | **Dara (female)** |
| "No" delivery | "mouths--*No*" | **"stands tall...smiles...*No*"** |
| Body metaphor | Absent | **Central** |
| Nose wound | Absent | **Present** (blood in courtroom) |
| Racial difference | Implicit | **Explicit** ("considerably fairer in skin than El") |

**D7 Ending** (Scene 129):
```
Dave puts his hand on El's shoulder, sighs, and just as he turns to say--

El stops him, urging him aside. She stands tall...

She smiles.

*No*.
```

**Transformation Analysis**: From "mouths" (D4) to "stands tall...smiles" (D7). The ending transforms from ambiguous vocalization attempt to triumphant physical assertion. El's body—previously dissociated, wounded, constrained—becomes the vehicle of her agency.

## PART IV: DRIFT ANALYSIS — WHAT CHANGED BETWEEN DRAFTS

### Change 1: Title Evolution

```
"A Girl and Her Dogs" → "Tale of El" → "El"
```

**Drift Pattern**: Progressive distillation from descriptive phrase to mythic register to essential noun.

**Interpretation**: The author moved from external framing (defining El by her dogs) to internal focus (El as subject) to minimal declaration (El as irreducible identity).

---

### Change 2: El's Age (14→16)

**Treatment/D4**: El is 14
**D7**: El is 16

**Possible Motivations**:
1. **Legal considerations**: 16-year-old protagonist avoids certain content restrictions
2. **Agency plausibility**: Older teen can more credibly conduct investigation
3. **Minimum wage reference**: D7 explicitly ties "sixteen" to "minimum-wage age"
4. **Sexual content calibration**: Scenes with Hugh and Julian/Julia less fraught with older protagonist

**Feedback Correlation**: No explicit feedback requested age change—this appears to be internal artistic decision.

---

### Change 3: Julian→Julia (Gender Transformation)

**Treatment/D4**: Julian (34), male Russian landlord, surveillance mansion
**D7**: Julia, female cult leader figure, chateau sequence

**Transformation Implications**:
- Male threat (predatory surveillance) → Female manipulation (false nurture)
- Direct dominance → Psychological entrapment
- Single location (mansion) → Extended sequence (chateau, swamp, escape)

**Feedback Correlation**: Nicholl readers did not see D4's Julian; the change was made before competition submission. Rowland feedback may have identified Julian as underdeveloped or clichéd.

**Authorial Intent Analysis**: The shift to female antagonist complicates simple gender dynamics. Julia operates through nurture-as-control—a more insidious mode of predation that implicates El's own desires (for mother figure, safety).

---

### Change 4: Daryl→Dara (Officer/Judge Gender Flip)

**D4**: "PROSECUTOR DARYL", "JUDGE DARYL", "STENOGRAPHER DARYL"
**D7**: "PROSECUTOR DARA", "JUDGE DARA", "STENOGRAPHER DARA"

**Parallel Feminization**: This change mirrors the Julian→Julia shift. Both authority figures (legal and criminal) transform from male to female.

**Effect**: The screenplay's power structures become less straightforwardly patriarchal. Women occupy positions of both protection (Dara) and predation (Julia), refusing simple gendered victimhood narrative.

---

### Change 5: The "No" Delivery Evolution

**Treatment**: "with her voice she says, 'no'"
**D4**: "She mouths--*No*"
**D7**: "She stands tall...She smiles. *No*."

**Evolution Analysis**:

| Version | Physical | Vocal | Affective |
|---------|----------|-------|-----------|
| Treatment | Unspecified | Explicit ("with her voice") | Unspecified |
| D4 | Seated ("sits up") | Ambiguous ("mouths") | Resistant |
| D7 | Triumphant ("stands tall") | Internal ("*No*.") | Joyful ("smiles") |

**Nicholl Reader 2's Observation**: "since 'No' is not written as dialogue, perhaps she just thinks this"

**Authorial Response**: The reader correctly identified the ambiguity—and the author **maintained it deliberately**. D7 intensifies rather than resolves the question of vocalization. The addition of "smiles" reframes the "No" from desperate resistance to empowered choice.

**Drift Pattern**: INTERNALIZATION OF AMBIGUITY. Rather than clarifying per feedback, the author deepened the interpretive challenge.

---

### Change 6: The Body Metaphor (Structural Innovation)

**Treatment/D4**: No "Body" device
**D7**: Central structural element

**The Body as Dissociation Device**:

D7 opens with "The Body" rather than "El"—third-person self-reference that signals trauma-induced dissociation. The protagonist perceives her own body as alien object.

**Resolution Arc**: By ending with "She stands tall...She smiles," El has reintegrated body and self. The Body metaphor creates a structural journey that D4 lacked.

**Feedback Correlation**: Neither Aaron, Adam, nor Rowland feedback is available for direct correlation, but the innovation suggests response to critique of psychological depth or structural coherence.

**Nicholl Response**: Reader 1 praised "real visual sense...flights of fancy." Reader 2 noted confusion about "real and what was...created in El's brain." The Body metaphor may have contributed to this confusion—or may have been an attempt to formalize the surreal elements that were previously inconsistent.

---

### Change 7: Race-Car Color (Yellow→Red)

**Treatment/D4**: Yellow Corvette/race-car
**D7**: RED race-car

**Symbolic Register Shift**: Yellow carries connotations of caution, cowardice, sickness. Red carries connotations of danger, blood, passion, urgency.

**Chromatic Intensification**: The shift to red aligns with D7's more visceral treatment of trauma (nose wound, blood in courtroom, swamp escape).

---

### Change 8: Explicit Racial Difference

**D4**: El's ethnicity implicit
**D7**: "Both are considerably fairer in skin than El"

**Addition Location**: Description of Sandra and Dave in Scene 2.

**Effect**: The fostering dynamic gains explicit racial dimension. El is visibly "other" in her adoptive home—her difference inscribed on her body.

**Thematic Resonance**: Connects to The Body metaphor. El's body is marked by both trauma (dissociation) and race (visible difference).

## PART VI: META-ANALYSIS — THE AUTHOR'S DRIFT PHILOSOPHY

### Observation 1: Voice Protection Over Clarity

The author consistently prioritized distinctive voice over accessible clarity. Nicholl readers praised "imaginative and confident" voice while critiquing comprehensibility. The author chose to protect the voice.

### Observation 2: Ambiguity as Feature, Not Bug

Reader 2's note that "'No' is not written as dialogue, perhaps she just thinks this" identifies the screenplay's central interpretive crux. The author **could have** clarified by making the "No" unambiguous dialogue. Instead, D7 **intensified** the ambiguity by adding "smiles"—transforming the moment from resistance to triumph while maintaining the question of vocalization.

This suggests the author views the ending's ambiguity as essential to meaning, not a defect to be corrected.

### Observation 3: Structural Innovation Over Plot Density

Readers consistently critiqued "plot-light" structure and "random encounters." The author responded not by adding plot but by adding **structural architecture** (The Body metaphor, Julia arc). The episodic quality was preserved but given psychological throughline.

### Observation 4: Gender Transformation as Thematic Deepening

The parallel gender flips (Julian→Julia, Daryl→Dara) suggest deliberate thematic work. The author moved from simple patriarchal villainy to complex female authority (both protective and predatory). This was not requested by any documented feedback—it appears to be internal artistic evolution.

### Observation 5: Ending Transformation as Central Concern

The most dramatic change across all drafts is the ending:

- Treatment: Explicit vocal "no"
- D4: Ambiguous mouthed "No"
- D7: Triumphant physical assertion + internal "No"

Each revision **intensifies** El's agency while **reducing** verbal expression. The author moved toward a vision of voice-as-selfhood rather than voice-as-speech.
