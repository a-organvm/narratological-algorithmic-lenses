## 1. "A Girl and Her Dogs" Era

### Draft D0 (2017-10-31) — Baseline Version

The screenplay opens with a flash-forward nightmare tableau: El, a young mute girl, crouches in bushes beside her dog Albie as police sirens assault the night, their lights beating against their faces in rhythm with the wail of emergency vehicles. The scene establishes immediate tension and fear, suggesting the entire narrative will pivot around a crime or crisis centered on their neighborhood.

The narrative then cuts to El at home with her adopted parents Sandra and Dave, who sit across from her in a formal configuration, taking a collective breath before delivering what feels like a significant announcement. This opening framing device circles back to the initial police presence, hinting that this family conversation concerns matters of life and death.

El's alarm clock defines her mundane reality: 6:00 AM, snoozed twice by 6:07 AM. She's reluctant to rise, uncertain about starting another day, though there's no choice but to proceed. From off-screen, Dave and Sandra's voices layer over this scene, establishing the family's financial and emotional crisis. Dave was "let go" from his job—he emphasizes the distinction from being "fired," a pedantic correction that reveals his need to maintain dignity in diminished circumstances. Sandra dismisses his careful semantics while explaining to El that money is tight. They acknowledge a "tough year" for El herself, though El's own sources of difficulty remain opaque at this narrative stage. Sandra and Dave speak to El as if preparing her for something significant, framing financial hardship as a family affair requiring everyone's participation and sacrifice.

The conversation pivots toward employment possibilities for El. Dave mentions bagging groceries at Publix, his voice hopeful about the modest wage and potential tips from elderly customers. Sandra suggests math tutoring, praising El's academic strength. When El shows no enthusiasm, Sandra finally mentions dog walking—a suggestion that visibly transforms El's demeanor. She nods emphatically and pulls out a deck of cards wrapped in rubber bands. Each card bears a handwritten phrase, allowing El to communicate through pre-written messages. She hands Dave a card reading "Sounds Good!"—a moment of agency and hope puncturing the financial anxiety.

El's daily existence unfolds in disciplined routine and determined isolation. At high school, during lunch period when peers cluster around smartphones and selfie-taking, El positions herself at the far end of the courtyard on a bench with her laptop and earbuds, visibly separate. She constructs a flyer for dog walking services, her focus absolute despite the social isolation surrounding her. Messages ping constantly from a group of girls—Mia, Emily, Alani, Andrea, and Chloe—but El ignores them, recognizing the false friendliness masking cruelty. The taunting messages themselves testify to her outsider status and their contempt.

The dog walking business develops through a series of quick-cut montages: El managing multiple leashes, dogs straining and pulling, park encounters with playful chaos, baths where Albie splashes her soaking wet, the complicated logistics of caring for numerous animals. She becomes visibly exhausted yet determined, pulling dozens of dogs through the streets, collecting payments from various clients. Gary, a construction worker in his 40s, asks her about feeding costs. She gains followers and testimonials praising her care. The work becomes her identity and purpose.

One crucial stop: Barb and Hugh's apartment. Hugh, an older New York transplant, rambles at exhausting length about his life—the superiority of NYC subways, his chronic complaining, his wife Barb who nags him to exercise, his difficult knee. His monologues are self-centered and tiresome, but his voice carries the quality of someone seeking engagement and attention, even through negativity. Barb, meanwhile, represents warmth and maternal care. She's concerned about her aging husband, defensive about her aging dog Madison, and genuinely touched by El's gentleness with the animal. When they can't pay El one week because Hugh is waiting for a check, Barb invites her to dinner as compensation, offering hospitality and connection.

The narrative's turning point arrives with El's encounter with Sheen. El hesitantly approaches Sheen's apartment door, nervous about knocking. Sheen opens it—a young Latina woman with artistic sensibility, sensuality, and obvious struggle beneath her surface charm. Sheen's dog, Albie (Albert Einstein), immediately bonds with El. Sheen invites El inside, and a genuine connection begins forming. They share food—Sheen's "ultra special mega spectacular Mac n cheese," a recipe passed down through generations, transforms El's palate and represents nurturing. Sheen's apartment becomes a sanctuary: her workspace radiates with graffiti art covering every surface, and El is astounded by the visual richness. Sheen mentions dreams of having her work displayed in Wynwood, Miami's prestigious art district, gesturing toward larger artistic ambitions.

However, Sheen's life carries darker undercurrents. El notices her leaving in the yellow Corvette, observed through blinds with suspicion. When El stays overnight, she discovers Sheen engaged in drug use—the specific imagery of crushing Percocet into powder form on a bill, rolling it, snorting the resulting line—reveals addiction driving Sheen's existence beneath the artistic persona. The friendly visits and intimate moments—nail painting, ice cream outings, watching films together in close proximity—contrast sharply with Sheen's private chemical dependency and apparent sex work or transactional relationships suggested by the parade of visitors Jeannie, their neighbor, loudly comments upon.

Jeannie herself becomes a character of consequence. Middle-aged, sharp-tongued, and living with Walter, a much younger man, she's positioned as the moral scold of the apartment complex. Her monologue to El about 9/11—she was in the Twin Towers that morning, missed her ferry, lost people, watched her firefighter husband Jeffrey deteriorate psychologically until he took his own life—adds tragic depth to her bitterness. She warns El against trying to save people from self-destruction, a warning tinged with personal wisdom about falling oneself. Despite her harshness about Sheen's sexuality and lifestyle, Jeannie's ultimate message is survival: don't slip trying to save others.

The narrative structure then executes a temporal break. One evening, El arrives at the apartment complex to find police lights, sirens, and emergency vehicles converging on Sheen's apartment. Dave emerges to inform El that Sheen is dead, the circumstances are "cut and dry," and El must find a new home for Albie. El closes the door on this information, collapsing onto her bed in despair, and Albie nuzzles her offering comfort.

The turning point transforms El's character arc. Instead of accepting the official narrative of overdose, El becomes a self-appointed investigator. She sets an alarm, sneaks out past her sleeping parents, and returns to Sheen's crime scene, crossing under police tape to document everything—photographs of the disrupted bedroom, drug paraphernalia, the intimate polaroids on the refrigerator revealing Sheen's social world. She methodically catalogs potential suspects: Gary appears prominently in photos despite being significantly older than Sheen's usual circle; Sal, who drives a yellow Corvette with a loud exhaust, appears multiple times; Walter, Jeannie's husband, shows up in party photos.

El's investigative methodology combines digital sophistication with detective work. She creates an Instagram account for Sheen, posting her artwork to build a following and generate interest. She compiles evidence—cropped faces from polaroids, drug baggie patterns—and posts these to her Tumblr asking followers for help identifying subjects and tracing connections. She constructs a questionnaire and prints laminated copies, methodically interviewing neighbors. She doctors police document requests with fake signatures, and she orchestrates an elaborate gathering of college kids next door to examine their memories of Sheen and potential visitors.

The college kids become El's investigative team. Teddy, Susan, Nicole, Omar, and Micah discuss the people in Sheen's photographs, identify Sal as their drug supplier, note his yellow Corvette and aggressive behavior. They acknowledge Gary's creepy interest in young women, recounting Nicole's account of him placing a hand on her lower back when she was 17, asking "And?" when she told him her age. The group dismisses Walter as too stupid and dependent on Jeannie's money to be dangerous. Their information points El toward Sal as the most likely suspect—a drug dealer with access to opiates, who spent time in the building, and who drives the distinctive vehicle El seeks to place at the crime scene.

However, the investigation produces ambiguity rather than clarity. When El confronts Officer Billy at the police station with her extensive evidence folder, he dismisses her findings. The official story is overdose: bruises on Sheen's inner elbows indicate intravenous drug use, and bruises on her neck are attributed to consensual choking during sex. The police have closed the case. El feels dismissed and invalidated.

When El's parents discover her investigation—Dave sees the desktop list of suspects displayed on her laptop—they intervene forcefully. In a formal family sitting, both parents lecture El about moving on, the dangers of conspiracy thinking, and her inappropriate investigation. Dave and Sandra reference real-world examples of conspiracy theories preventing grieving parents from processing loss. They recognize that Sheen, while seemingly cool, was ultimately a cautionary tale of self-destruction through drug addiction. They set firm boundaries: El must cease all investigation, stop pestering neighbors, stop cutting school. The compromise is allowing her to keep Albie, a concession framed as recognition of the dog's beneficial impact on her mental health.

Yet El's investigation continues, now clandestinely. She researches lock-picking techniques and breaks into Gary's apartment while he's away. She cares for his two cats—feeding, watering, cleaning litter—revealing her moral nature even while committing a felony. In his bedside drawer, she discovers drug baggies matching the design of those found in Sheen's apartment. Gary has become her primary suspect.

The surveillance component deepens when Dave mentions being paid by the landlord to clean out Sheen's apartment. El demands that the artwork not be discarded, and she physically removes one piece, preserving Sheen's creative legacy. Meanwhile, she's received parking lot footage from the building—submitted as a police evidence request—and has identified that Gary's truck disappeared around the time between Sheen's death and her body's discovery, matching the autopsy timeline.

El confronts Sal through text messages while he drives alongside her in his yellow Corvette. He picks her up at gunpoint, holding a pistol on her in the car. He admits he didn't supply Sheen with opiates that night but doesn't deny knowledge of her drug use or his sexual relationship with her ("five dollars short and she'd swallow"). The interrogation reveals that Sal is dangerous but claims innocence in Sheen's death. El leaves the car and continues her investigation.

The ending reveals that El's investigation has achieved a different kind of success. Sheen's Instagram/blog account has gone viral, accumulating thousands of followers. A major Miami gallery, impressed by the work, extends an exhibition offer in Wynwood—the very dream Sheen expressed early in the narrative. However, El declines the offer on Sheen's behalf, understanding that the artistic legacy, rather than a sensationalized murder narrative, represents the truest memorial to her friend's existence and potential. The screenplay fades to black with El smiling, having redirected her investigative energy toward preserving Sheen's artistic memory rather than pursuing questions of justice that the adult authorities have deemed settled.

The baseline draft presents a narrative about adolescent activism, grief, the unreliability of official narratives, and the choice between pursuing vengeance and preserving memory. El remains mute throughout, communicating through written cards, digital text, and observation—her silence paradoxically making her the film's most perceptive character. The dog-walking profession becomes a vehicle for independence, economic agency, and unexpected intimacy with neighborhood adults. The relationship with Sheen—brief, transformative, and tragic—propels El from isolated teenager into investigator, though the investigation ultimately matters less than the artistic resurrection.

### Draft D1.5 (2017-11-28) — Darkened Version

The darkened version begins identically but immediately diverges in tone and content, establishing an entirely different thematic universe. The opening police sirens remain, but the narrative immediately flashes backward through a sequence of horrific images: Sheen's naked, bloodied corpse crammed into the trunk of a yellow Corvette, her neck bruised and bound; Sheen's wrist sliced vertically—"not sideways for attention but longways for results"—as a hand with nail polish guides a pen to forge a suicide note; Sheen slumped over her bed, nose and mouth bloodied, a foaming drink spilled beside her, her body convulsing as she dies while a shadow of a naked man looms in the background. These flash cuts establish that Sheen's death is a murder presented as suicide—a crucial narrative distinction. The shadow man, obscured and undefined, becomes the driving mystery motivating El's investigation.

The second draft emphasizes El's specific cultural identity as a young Seminole girl, noted parenthetically but thematically significant. This detail anchors her within indigenous history and suggests particular vulnerability within systems of power and authority. Her muteness carries greater weight as enforced silence, distinguishing between her chosen communication method (written cards, digital text) and the silence imposed by her biology and identity.

The family conversation maintains its bones but gains new texture. Dave and Sandra explicitly tell El that money is tight, that they need her help, and that they're treating her "like an adult." The financial crisis feels more dire and intimate. Dave's references to his own working-class history—his grandfather forcing his father to dig holes as character building, his own childhood job emptying septic tanks—frame work as essential masculinity and self-respect. He begins early, repeatedly asserting that El needs to learn independence. This insistent framing suggests that El's labor is not negotiable kindness but familial necessity.

The scene of El's morning routine gains a bathroom setting. She's brushing teeth and performing bodily care while her parents' voices layer over, emphasizing her burgeoning adolescence and bodily autonomy. She checks her popular blog and, notably, reviews Mia's blog—her bully's social media—suggesting El monitors her antagonizer's activities, a dynamic that complicates notions of bullying as one-directional.

El's window-view reveals the neighborhood with darker implications. She observes Gary directing undocumented migrant workers into vehicles for work, suggesting exploitation and illegality operating openly in her environment. She watches Sheen exit the yellow Corvette—a car associated with drug dealing throughout the narrative—as if Sheen's visible movements in the parking lot already mark her as involved in illicit activity.

The high school scene introduces a shocking fantasy sequence absent from the baseline. After the girls taunt El and one closes her laptop, the narrative cuts to an extended dream/revenge fantasy: El, dressed in military garb and boots, stands before women lined up against a wall with burlap sacks over their heads, their throats tied with yarn in exaggerated red bows. El shoots them systematically, unloading an entire clip, killing all of them in slow motion before blowing smoke from the barrel like a cowgirl. This fantasy violence expresses El's rage at bullying in visceral, cinematic terms—she's not passive but fantasizes violent retribution. The nightmare fantasy emphasizes the psychological toll of adolescent cruelty and El's capacity for murderous ideation, no matter how internally confined.

Following school, El stops at a bakery where she meets Sharon, the baker. Sharon is explicitly religious, evangelical, and concerned for El's spiritual welfare. She repeatedly mentions mass, the priest who asks about El, and her conviction that the church will "change your life." A cut reveals Sharon in religious ecstasy, speaking in tongues and being administered religious rites, suggesting either authentic spiritual conviction or performative zealotry. The exchange between Sharon's kindness and her insistent religiosity—combined with the later revelation that Sharon is judgmental about Sheen's visible tattoos and body autonomy—complicates notions of community support.

Through the bus window, El observes Sheen looking "worse for wear," suggesting visible signs of drug use or degradation not apparent in the baseline version. When Sheen later introduces herself, there's immediate chemistry but also darker undertones. Sheen's apartment becomes a sanctuary, but the work sequences reveal her using the bathroom, crushing pills in her mouth, counting cash, and texting on her phone in ways that suggest transactional sex work rather than casual dating.

The critical scene occurs when Dave arrives at Sheen's apartment for a repair. He's carrying a wrench and explains he "fixed your toilet." But immediately, the scene shifts into surreal, disturbing territory. Dave's monologue becomes incoherent and self-degrading: "Whoever said there ain't no one whose shit don't stink… I'm pathetic and need to go out of town for a thing I don't care about because my wife owns me. Watch our retarded daughter, please? We can't pay you anything because we don't have any money." He laughs and then cries, crawling toward Sheen and licking her toes. The scene suggests either Dave's sexual desperation, psychological breakdown, or some transactional arrangement where El's childcare is bartered for marital intimacy or emotional regulation.

The discovery of Sheen's drug use maintains its visual specificity: she's in lotus position, pulling three Percocets from her bra, crushing one into powder using a dollar bill and lighter. The act is clinical and habitual, suggesting deep addiction.

A stark cut shows El and Hugh on public transit, and Hugh's monologue explodes into pages of rambling, associative narrative. He discusses buses, the superiority of NYC subways, his exercise walks, his trip to Publix grocery store, and his complicated marriage to Barb, who nags him but whom he loves. His speech is incoherent, circling endlessly, digressive, punctuated by complaints about everything from lox quality to age. The screenplay incorporates extended dark fantasy sequences within Hugh's monologue: he's shown power-walking, his mechanical leg propelling him forward while terrifying him as his body becomes a machine beyond his control; he's in a supermarket where everyone he encounters recoils from him as if he's infected, spreading disease.

The fantasies continue with grotesque domestic scenarios: Hugh eating from a dog bowl while Barb feeds their dog Madison from the dinner table; Hugh lying paraplegic in bed while Barb smothers him with a pillow; Hugh's dead wife Barb's ashes in an urn on the kitchen table, the urn lid opening and closing as she nags him while he eats, until he throws the urn against the wall and suffocates in her dispersed remains.

This sequence introduces a secondary narrative strain: Hugh and Barb as a marriage of mutual resentment, bodily degradation, and fantasized violence. Their storyline carries implications of elder abuse, domestic violence, and the cruelties accumulating in long-term partnerships. When El visits their apartment, Hugh is indeed depicted beating Madison with his newspaper while Barb plays a dutiful wife, canned laughter punctuating scenes of him threatening to "knock you the fuck out" and joking about his first wife (implying her death). The canned laughter—entirely absent from the baseline—transforms the scenes into dark sitcom parody, suggesting American sitcom conventions normalize and laugh at domestic violence.

When El attempts to access the police station, she doesn't have a productive conversation with Officer Billy. Instead, she "storms out of the Police Station. She stomps her feet, flips around and flips off the entire building"—a gesture of explicit rejection of institutional authority entirely absent in the baseline version.

The investigation gains a sexual threat dimension absent from the baseline. When El meets with Julian, the apartment complex owner—revealed as an Eastern European man with a scarred scalp—the interaction turns darkly predatory. Julian speaks to El in deliberately menacing terms: "In my country, a little girl who can't open her mouth like you would be worth a lot of money under one condition, you can still at least moan and moan loud... If she could not moan or cry or scream or beg, she would've been drowned by her mother and thrown into a hole to be forgotten." He guides her into a security room and deliberately stands close, placing his arm around her and moving his hand toward her breasts. El bites his hand and flees. The encounter establishes that El, as a vulnerable girl, is subject to sexual predation from men in positions of authority—Julian literally owns the building and holds surveillance footage as leverage over her investigation.

The drug dealer Sal, when confronted, doesn't just deny involvement—he pulls up alongside her in the yellow Corvette and forces her into the car at gunpoint, traumatizing her with direct criminal threat and implied sexual danger ("I've killed people before... Five dollars short and she'd swallow").

The family intervention scene maintains its structure but gains new darkness. When Dave and Sandra tell El to abandon the investigation, Sandra's recriminations become far more hostile. She directly blames Dave for Sheen's death ("You killed her, didn't ya?") and launches into a monologue about his sexual inadequacy and infidelity ("Spineless. You always wanted to fuck her, fuck anything that walks. Couldn't control yourself?"). The implication is that Dave had some sexual or transactional relationship with Sheen, and Sandra holds him responsible for her death. A cut shows Dave sitting in the corner "whimpering" while Sandra, "on all fours, screaming, and wagging her finger," verbally assaults him. The domestic violence extends from Hugh and Barb's household into El's own home.

The ending maintains its structure—Sheen's work goes viral, the Wynwood offer arrives, El declines it—but the overall narrative has been transformed. Rather than a coming-of-age story about an adolescent discovering investigative agency and choosing artistic legacy over vengeance, the darkened version becomes a story about a vulnerable girl navigating systems of exploitation, predation, and male violence. Her muteness shifts from communicative choice to enforced silence in a world that would prefer her not to speak. Her investigation, while framed as detective work, is actually a desperate adolescent attempting to understand the predatory systems surrounding her—systems that include her own father's sexual desperation, her neighbors' domestic violence, institutional authorities' indifference, and criminal predators' threats. The dog-walking profession provides economic survival in a household facing financial crisis, not independent empowerment.

The darkened version maintains narrative ambiguity about Sheen's death—whether it's genuinely murder by an unidentified "shadow man," whether Dave's involvement constitutes murder, whether the system itself is implicated in her death through enabling predatory relationships. The flash cuts of Sheen's corpse in multiple scenarios suggest simultaneous truths or the impossibility of determining a single truth. The ending—El declining the art show—carries different weight in this darker context. Rather than noble memorialization, it might represent El's understanding that Sheen cannot be resurrected or saved, that the artistic legacy is all that survives systematic predation and male violence.

## 2. "Tale of El" Era

### Draft D2 (2017-12-08) — Title Change and Structural Professionalization

The screenplay opens with a striking noir-inflected inciting incident: mute fourteen-year-old Seminole girl El and her dog Albie witness a police raid on their apartment complex, where officers barge into an apartment above them while they hide in the bushes. This traumatic scene is immediately cross-cut with a horrifying revelation—the trunk of a yellow Corvette containing the dead body of Sheen, a young Latina woman with bruises around her neck and blood covering her body, gagged and tied hog-style. The opening further splinters into flashback sequences showing the multiple ways Sheen died: on a toilet with slashed wrists, a suicide note being written by a mysterious hand with manicured nails, on her bed convulsing and foaming from her mouth with a shadowed naked man lingering in the background. This fragmented opening establishes the film's central mystery—was Sheen's death suicide or murder?—while introducing El not as an active agent but as a traumatized witness to community violence.

The narrative immediately shifts into El's domestic morning routine at home with her adopted parents Sandra and Dave, who inform her they're financially struggling and need her help. Rather than moralizing, the screenplay presents the conversation as a genuine family discussion about responsibility. Sandra and Dave present a list of job options. When they finally suggest "dog walker," El's face lights up, and she produces a deck of communication cards (handwritten phrases on one side, blank playing cards on the other) to signal her enthusiastic agreement with "Sounds Good!"—a touching detail that establishes her mute status as a fact of her life rather than a source of tragedy.

What follows is an extended middle section—the true heart of the D2 draft—that functions almost as a separate coming-of-age narrative set apart from the murder mystery. The screenplay establishes El's high school world: she sits alone at lunch creating flyers on her laptop while bullies led by Mia taunt her mercilessly. There's a stylized fantasy sequence where El, in military garb, executes Mia and her cronies firing a pistol into their bodies as they hang against a wall—a psychic release from the daily humiliation. But when the bullies actually confront El on the playground, she responds with an unexpected embrace of Mia before pushing past her with cryptic kindness and a wave.

The dog-walking business becomes a genuine narrative arc. El begins with one dog, then two, then accumulates seven: Albie, Madison, Tozer, Gabriel Magnus, Princess Victoria, and Boo. The screenplay montages her collecting payment, giving baths, playing at parks, dealing with difficult clients. She stays at Barb and Hugh's apartment where the elderly couple engage in affectionate bickering; Hugh is a cantankerous retired Jewish man from New York who regales anyone nearby with long, rambling monologues about his life, his ailing wife Barb, his old neighborhood, and his dignity. There's a sideshow where we see Hugh's fantasy life: his power-walking destroying his body, his wife threatening to murder him with a pillow, him eating from a dog bowl while Barb feeds their dog at the table.

The centerpiece relationship develops between El and Sheen (revealed to be alive when El first knocks on her door). Sheen lives in an apartment near the college kids; she's an artist who spray-paints graffiti canvases. When El arrives to care for Albie, Sheen invites her inside, and they bond over Mac and cheese, homework, and art. Sheen's dark secret—she snorts crushed Percocet pills—is shown in a quiet scene where she carefully crushes the drug. But the relationship genuinely deepens. They paint together, sneak glimpses out the window at the yellow Corvette coming and going (Sheen briefly leaves to visit it), paint their nails together, go for ice cream, watch films in bed.

The third act becomes El's amateur detective work after Sheen's death. She sneaks back into Sheen's apartment beneath crime scene tape and systematically photographs everything: the bloodstained sheets, drug baggies, polaroids of various people at parties. She creates a blog for Sheen's art, posts evidence photos asking followers for help, interviews neighbors with a questionnaire she's printed and laminated, and meticulously files everything in a folder labeled "Evidence."

The supporting characters each receive interviews: Sharon, the religious bakery owner, who shares her suspicions about Sheen's loose morals while simultaneously having a hallucinatory vision of herself as a cult member sacrificing Sheen on an altar. Jeannie, the older woman on her second marriage to the much younger Walter, reveals her traumatic history as a 9/11 survivor whose first husband, a firefighter, committed suicide in their basement years later. Through the college kids (Teddy, Sue, Nicole, Omar, Micah), El learns about Sal, who delivers drugs in a yellow Corvette and seems connected to Sheen. She also learns Gary, the creepy middle-aged construction worker, frequents the parties and preys on young women.

El creates suspects: Sal (the drug dealer), Walter (the trophy husband potentially after Jeannie's 9/11 insurance money), and Gary (the predatory older man). But when she goes to meet the mysterious Julian (landlord and building owner) at his mansion, he intimidates and sexually harasses her before providing parking lot surveillance footage. Gary's truck disappears around the time of Sheen's death, and the surveillance shows him fleeing.

The climax involves El confronting Sal in his yellow Corvette at gunpoint, where he admits he wasn't the supplier that night so had no reason to visit Sheen, though he confesses to their transactional sexual relationship where she'd perform sex acts for five dollars or less. Sandra and Dave intervene, sitting El down and forbidding her investigation, insisting Sheen killed herself with drugs and that conspiracy theories are harmful.

But the ending is ambiguous and bittersweet. El maintains her innocence while secretly continuing her investigation. Sheen's blog goes viral with her artwork receiving ten thousand followers. Miami's Wynwood district offers to feature one of Sheen's paintings on their prestigious public art walls. El receives the email offer and reads it aloud—the first time we hear her actual voice in the entire screenplay—then declines the offer on Sheen's behalf, recognizing that true justice and memorialization lie in honoring Sheen's legacy as an artist rather than solving her death.

**Title Significance:** The shift from "A Girl and Her Dogs" to "Tale of El" is fundamental to understanding the screenplay's artistic intention. The original title suggests a heartwarming coming-of-age story about entrepreneurship and pet care. "Tale of El" explicitly positions El as the mythic center of the narrative, resonating with literary traditions of the quest tale (El as seeker, investigator, truth-teller). The muteness that could be read as a limitation becomes, through the title change, a distinctive mark of her narrative identity.

### Drafts D2.5-D3 (December 2017-January 2018) — Refinements

The D2.5 and D3 drafts maintain the core D2 structure while introducing meaningful refinements:

**Racial/Cultural Positioning:** El is now described as a "mute Latina" rather than "Seminole," with parenthetical notes clarifying that her dialogue appears as subtitles on screen. This racial repositioning may reflect the writer's research or creative revision regarding representation.

**Critical Addition (D3):** When the police barge in during the opening, a crucial detail emerges in the flashback to Sheen's death on the toilet: "CAMERA PULLS BACK revealing the backside of a woman in RED DRESS as she bends down to grab Sheen's legs and then begins to drag her dead body off the toilet, onto the floor, and out of the bathroom." This transformative revelation—Sheen's death may involve a woman—complicates the male-perpetrator assumption.

**Character Deepening:** Hugh's racial stereotyping becomes more explicit ("filthy Japs," "holier than thou Californians"), while Barb adds explicitly racist commentary: "Probably got some white in him, I bet. I always said: blacks need some white in them to ease those large features." This casual racism deepens characterization as religious piety coexisting with bigotry.

**Fraternity Scene:** The college party scene now explicitly identifies the punch-bowl drugging participants as fraternity members in Greek letters, adding institutional sexual predation to the narrative.

**Julian's Escalation:** Julian's predatory monologue adds: "Or maybe burned" after his threat about drowning girls who cannot moan, escalating the explicit threat of violence and trafficking.

**Biological Parents Revelation:** D3 adds crucial context when Dave says Sheen "killed herself. She took too many drugs and killed herself too." The "too" creates ambiguity about whether he means Sheen's death mirrors El's biological mother's (implied drug-related), and Sandra adds: "Like your parents. That's what drug addicts do." This reveals El's biological parents died from drug addiction, making her resistance to accepting Sheen's official death narrative deeply personal.

## 3. Treatment Document

### Treatment (2018-08-15) — 117 Scenes, Authorial Intent Baseline

The treatment for EL presents a surreal, darkly comedic coming-of-age narrative set in a disorienting landscape where suburban realism collides with fever-dream unreality. The film opens with El—fourteen-year-old—caught in vines as tropical vegetation swallows her, police sirens flashing across her panicked face. This haunting image establishes the tone: something has gone catastrophically wrong.

We are then pulled back into mundane domesticity. El, now the subject of an intervention by her step-parents Sandra (43) and Dave (39), sits on a couch, phone in hand. The morning sun streams through blinds. Her parents awkwardly explain that she needs to find part-time work. Sandra wears formal attire; Dave remains in pajamas, both shuffling through folders of paperwork. When a dog fight erupts nearby, El suggests via text: "dog walking?" This becomes her escape route from the suffocating pressure of family expectations.

The world El inhabits is populated by grotesque and compelling secondary characters. Gary (29), a violent man with a pit bull, materializes in the parking lot orchestrating brutality against migrant workers. The yellow race-car driven by Frank (27)—entirely dressed in head-to-toe yellow leather—arrives with his passenger Sheen (23), a woman who exists between indecent exposure and reckless abandon. Frank's attention is drawn to El through his phone; he texts her: "what're you looking at?" The encounter is charged with menace barely masked by adolescent swagger.

At school, El builds dog-walking flyers on her laptop during recess. The narrative flickers with surrealist flourishes: she paints with a brush double her size onto a canvas the size of North America, gargantuan waves crashing at its sides. When a spiked volleyball collides with her, she and her laptop go flying into a parked car, which slides into the street. On a beach, five female uniformed bullies (led by Mia, 15) surround her, taunting her to volleyball. Emily (15) stands apart from the others. El dons a uniform of opposing colors, enters the court, and almost immediately drops the ball, pivots, and walks away. Mia yells after her, telling her to return when she's ready to face them.

The film's logic becomes increasingly unstable. In a Publix supermarket, El encounters Hugh (78), described as "the local New York Jewish zombie," stumbling down an aisle. She flees in terror. At a bakery that transforms into a televangelized church, Sharon (51) shames Sheen for her tattoos and offensive outfits, calling for the pastor to exorcize her sins. Sheen laughs, unfazed, and passes El a casual "good luck!"

On the bus, El keeps her distance from Sheen, who sits at the back. She watches Hugh terrorize the bus driver, then nervously observes as he beckons her over. El surrenders to his gravitational pull and sits nearby. Hugh lectures her on the hardship of his life in rambling, relentless monologue. They eventually disembark, El carrying most of his grocery bags while pedestrians flee from them as if he were poisonous.

The Sheen relationship develops through several key scenes: El's reluctance to knock, Sheen's welcoming chaos, Mac and cheese that El cannot eat (lactose intolerant), the art room revelation, and the collaborative painting session. A white stag bursts through the window, leading to a surreal hunting sequence in Floridian swampland where Sheen has the stag in her sights but lets it escape: "I like the painting the way it is."

Frank's threat escalates when El witnesses him enter Sheen's apartment with his own key, then flee. Police and ambulance converge. Sheen's covered body is wheeled out. El begins her investigation: the polaroids, the bloody sheets, the suspect list. She emails a journalist, visits Julian's fortress where armed guards and KGB officers roam. Julian drugs her, shows her surveillance of her own apartment, forces her to watch violent pornography on floor-to-ceiling screens. El escapes.

The trial sequence materializes in El's living room. Each suspect interviews El; she declines all of them over and over, a cacophony of non-consent building. Barb and Hugh give their piece despite having no stake. Officer Bob conducts the courtroom with Albie as judge. Frank reveals his softer side. Mia is upset that El touched her. Gary makes his appearance. The officer takes the stand and lets El know she faces surmountable crimes.

**The Critical Ending:** When everyone's gone, it's just the three of them and the dog, posed just as they were at the beginning. They tell El she must get rid of Albie.

And with her voice, she says: "no."

This treatment establishes a fundamentally surreal, tone-shifting narrative where El moves from pre-verbal paralysis through active investigation and resistance to final vocal defiance. The authorial voice maintains dark comedy throughout—sardonic asides, grotesque visual humor, surrealist intrusions. The "no" is explicitly vocalized, representing the baseline authorial intent for El's journey toward vocal emancipation.

## 4. "El" Era: Early Phase

### Draft D4 (2018-09-26) — Surrealist Restructure

**The Surrealist Opening and El's World**

The Fourth Draft establishes El as a fourteen-year-old caught between states of being—alive yet suffocating, compliant yet resistant. The opening presents a teenager slouched on a couch while parasitic vines creep from beneath it, their tendrils wrapping around her ankles and climbing toward her throat. This botanical invasion runs throughout the domestic scene as Sandra and Dave announce El's expulsion from private school. The vines are not mere metaphor but tangible threat, choking her as papers from manila folders engulf the apartment like a blizzard, turning parental concern into slapstick chaos. El's world is one of constant low-level horror punctuated by absurdist bureaucratic cruelty.

The early sequences depict El navigating a dystopian Miami: a checkout clerk named Hugh spreads casual racism on buses, a pit bull owner releases his dog on migrant workers, Frank pursues El in a yellow race car while firing bullets at her school, and the natural world itself—weather systems, animals—moves with surreal hostility. Yet El moves through this wreckage with detached observation, her silence treating the chaos as simply the texture of existence. She produces hand-written cards from her pocket to communicate, carries a dog-walking flyer design, and observes the other teenagers and adults around her with the precision of a natural historian documenting specimens in a hostile ecosystem.

**The Chateau Sequence and Julian's Dominion**

Following Sheen's death—discovered through a news segment where Frank broadcasts grief and accusation—El becomes an investigator. She collects evidence, drafts emails to reporters, and eventually receives a phone call from Julian, an unseen voice inviting her to "his office" at midnight if she wants to know what happened to her friend. The journey to Julian's compound becomes a descent into surrealist nightmare.

The chateau sequence begins with a rideshare vehicle traveling endless driveways lined with Christmas lights, entering a fog-shrouded fortress protected by a moat, a drawbridge, and armed guards. A prehistoric alligator snaps at El as she crosses the bridge; Alek, Julian's massive enforcer, wrestles a momma gator and throws it back into the swamp. The mansion itself is a hall of horrors: inside, KGB officers march in drills, sports cars line the driveway, and the competing music of Russian opera and 808 bass war for dominance.

El navigates the mansion's interior—a strobe-lit maze of doors leading to increasingly disturbing revelations. Behind one door lies a nightclub filled only with women. Behind another, the glimpse of restraints, duct tape, and a scream. When she finds Julian descending the grand staircase in silk robes, he presents himself as a gracious host: cultured, attentive, almost paternal. He escorts her through a red velvet secret passageway to a surveillance room where wall-mounted television screens broadcast feeds from El's apartment complex, her parents sleeping, her life reduced to pixels.

Julian forces water down her throat—"crushed and put in the water," he explains—and begins showing her screens cycling through moans and cries, pleasure indistinguishable from pain. When he excuses himself to prepare his bedroom and orders Alek to set up cameras, El makes her escape: she steals Julian's remote control, stabs Alek's eyes with her thumbs, locks both men in the secret passageway, and flees across the swamp bridge, tossing the remote to the alligators.

**The Courtroom Absurdism and All "Daryl"**

What unfolds is a legal nightmare where every authority figure—judge, prosecutor, officer—shares the same name: Daryl. The courtroom becomes a surrealist performance of justice gone haywire.

Officer Daryl presides as judge; Prosecutor Daryl presents the state's case against El for "libel, disturbing a crime scene, not respecting elders or law, not going to church, and not responding to parents in a timely manner." The evidence Hugh provides—that El said "no" to an elderly man's request for help—becomes grounds for maximum sentencing. Prosecutor Daryl positions El as a liar, a faker, a child playing games while her betters investigate real crimes. The jury consists entirely of plainclothes Daryl clones who pass El's hand-written "not guilty" card down the line with mounting suspicion.

The courtroom sequence exemplifies the Fourth Draft's central vision: authority is theatrical, justice is performative, and El's only power is refusal. She mouths "No"—just "No"—at Sandra's final demand that Albie be gone within a week. The film ends not with verdict but with that single negation hanging in the domestic space.

**Key Difference from Treatment:** Julian remains male; "No" is mouthed rather than spoken.

### Julia Fragment (2019-03-27) — Character Exploration

The Julia fragment is a short, intensely cinematic sequence that presents El's encounter with an angelic/demonic figure in a pre-industrial chateau. The opening mirrors the later full drafts but inverts the power dynamic: instead of El arriving at a villain's mansion, Julia—described as being in her twenties but carrying an otherworldly quality—descends a grand staircase in flowing robes while a halo hovers around her head.

The halo immediately shifts: it becomes a crown of thorns. This transformation from divine light to instruments of suffering establishes Julia as a liminal figure, neither purely angel nor demon, but existing in the space between salvation and torture. The atmosphere is thick with religious imagery—the dome above is painted with Christian idols, the chandelier is massive and ecclesiastical—yet the space feels corrupted, sacred architecture repurposed for profane ends.

Julia commands El's body through proximity and force of will. She cups El's cheeks and El "involuntarily stands," drawn upward by Julia's touch. When Julia says "Let's eat," the command carries absolute authority. They enter a dining room dominated by a long wooden ornate table set with empty expensive silverware. Two chairs sit in the middle; ten barely-clad women occupy the remaining seats, sitting on their hands, their bodies rigid with fear or obedience.

Julia pours water from a metallic pitcher into a wine glass for El—water that tastes bitter and foul. Julia forces the entire glass into El's mouth with a "manicured fingertip," then explains: "A little bitter. But you get used to it." The water carries the same drug-infused substance that Julian would later force-feed El; here it manifests as Julia's casual control mechanism, normalization of violation.

Julia reveals her success and pride: she has created an empire, taken care of her brothers, built an importing and exporting business that has exceeded her wildest ambitions. Yet even as she boasts, her power is expressed through cruelty. She asks personal questions designed to invade—"Did you ever get your head stuck between railings? Little Chad ever try and play doctor with you?"—and when she learns of El's name, insists that "It's her name to give," referring to El's deceased mother, whose death Julia frames as beneficial: "In most ways, we're glad she's dead. She helped you stand out... She brought you to us."

The apostles—women named Juda and Petra and a chorus of unnamed followers—surround El, caressing her arms and legs, running hands through her hair. Under the influence of the water, El experiences "bliss for the first time; a careless bliss." She laughs without regard while enveloped by writhing bodies, her consciousness fragmenting into hallucination.

Yet even this moment of false transcendence is interrupted. The apostles vanish and El faces a bay of screens from floor to ceiling, each tuned to a different camera angle of her apartment complex. The surveillance reveal is total—Julia has watched not just El's body but her entire domestic existence.

The fragment ends with El's violent escape: she bites into Alek's gloved hand, blood pouring, kicks him down the entire staircase, and flees into emptiness.

### Draft D5 (2019-04-07) — Body Metaphor Introduced

**The Opening: Prescription Bottle and Corporeal Dissolution**

The Fifth Draft begins with a radical departure: the opening exists within the body itself. A prescription bottle's top slides open, revealing a "long and deep blackhole" at its bottom. A heart rate monitor chirps at 70 BPM, then accelerates to 120 as the sound draws near. A spotlight illuminates a "slender, hairless, and cocked arm fading in fighting an invisible force for its life" before cranking open like a drawbridge. A butterfly needle darts into the crook of the tensed arm at the speed of light.

The entire opening sequence exists in medical hyperreality—blue walls, sterile spaces, the intrusion of a needle into willing or unwilling flesh. The camera—or consciousness—gradually pulls back, establishing an INT. BATHROOM where a translucent, feature-less humanoid body showers. Water beats on its back. A heavenly light opens briefly; the figure turns to see but misses it, seeing only its own reflection in the smartphone on the counter.

The body assembles itself into specificity: El, age sixteen (changed from fourteen), dresses in "the uniform"—yellow t-shirt, jean shorts, sports bra. The body now has context, history, specificity. When the bathroom door bursts open with three loud knocks, El's "shoulder-length, pin-straight, black, and wet hair" swings toward the sound. She "screams without making a sound," her body contorting silently while her phone reveals a notification that prompts her smile.

**The Body Under Siege: Vines and Violation**

The surrealist opening of Draft 4 returns when El arrives in the living room, but now it is explicitly corporeal. Vines "of all different shapes and sizes" crawl from beneath the couch and wrap around El's "ankles." As Sandra and Dave argue about her school transfer, the vines wrap around her "calfs, then clutch her knees." She swipes at her knees, "aggravating them further." When the parents discuss employment options, the vines "secure El's waist to the couch, and crawl further up toward her chest." Most disturbingly, the vines "wrap around El's throat, and crawl up her cheeks," then "probe El's mouth, then force the jaw open to crawl down her throat, choking her." Her "fingers grip the sofa, digging into the fabric, and then tearing through to the cushion underneath."

The body is under siege from within the home itself. The physical environment has become parasitic, invading El's flesh, choking her vocal cords, assaulting her. This corporeal language persists throughout the Fifth Draft.

**The Julia Character (formerly Julian)**

Julia (20) replaces Julian. She descends a staircase with a halo that transforms into a crown of thorns. Religious imagery is significantly expanded. The Apostles—named characters Uda and Petra—appear among Julia's followers.

**Frank's Vehicle:** Red race-car (changed from yellow).

**Counselor Evolution:** Explicit transformation into eight-legged spider-human hybrid during confrontation.

**Ending:** El mouths "No"—the word silent but emerging from a body that has earned the right to speak it through sheer endurance.

## 5. "El" Era: Final Phase

### Draft D6 (2019-04-22) — Mia Expansion, Daryl→Dara

The Sixth Draft opens with a pharmaceutical and bodily metaphor framing the entire narrative. El, a non-verbal sixteen-year-old girl adopted eight years prior, inhabits a world where her body becomes a canvas for external control and intervention. The opening sequences establish her silence not as pathology but as agency, a deliberate absence of speech that distinguishes her from a world of meaningless noise.

**The Western Gunslinger Sequence**

The narrative's midpoint pivot arrives when El ventures into a Western gunslinger sequence of surprising tonal violence. Mia, a school antagonist introduced earlier, manifests as an armed rival in a desert town square. Here, El becomes a figure of measured restraint: when offered a gun, she tosses it aside. When Mia threatens her, El embraces her—a gesture of radical non-violence that invites brief reciprocity before Mia's defenses harden. Critically, Emily—a minor character advocating for restraint—attempts to intervene, standing between Mia and El's departing figure. Mia's gun fires. Emily falls, dying. El continues walking toward the sunset, untouched and unrepentant.

This sequence crystallizes El's ethical position: she refuses to escalate, yet catastrophe finds the innocent around her. Emily's death becomes the necessary sacrifice in a world El cannot control through gentleness.

**Julia's Backstory**

Julia's backstory emerges through atmospheric accumulation rather than exposition. She is Mylo and Alek's sister—the oligarchic operator of a chateau fortress deep in the swamp. Her entrance is baroque: she descends a grand staircase wearing robes with a halo that morphs into a crown of thorns. Julia operates a syndicate built on pharmaceutical distribution and human trafficking.

Julia's father's disappointment is revealed: "I'm dying...I wanted a boy."

**Authority Figure Feminization**

Judge/Officer now "Dara" (female) instead of "Daryl" (male). This feminization of institutional authority complicates simple gendered readings of systemic violence.

**The Bleeding Nostril**

Julia's final gesture before El's escape—slicing open El's nostril with a small blade—initiates a physical wound that persists through the narrative's conclusion. The wound appears and reappears throughout her escape sequence and aftermath—a constant reminder of Julia's invasion, a leakage of interior damage made visible.

**The "No" Evolution**

Earlier in the draft, El's refusal—her "No" spoken aloud to various demands—exists as mouthed language, physical resistance. By the trial sequence, her "No" becomes internalized, a pure assertion of refusal without vocalization. When confronted by the judge character (Dara), El's defense is to stand and shake her head "No!" with violent intensity. The word transforms from spoken utterance to embodied negation, a refusal that requires no voice to register its force.

**Emily's Fate:** Survives in D6—her death occurs only in the gunslinger fantasy sequence, existing as cautionary metaphor rather than literal fact.

### Draft D7 (2019-08-01) — Final Major Revision

The Seventh Draft refines Draft 6's architecture while introducing decisive tonal and thematic shifts. The pharmaceutical metaphor persists, but deepens into an examination of how bodies become property—how El's very flesh becomes contested terrain between those who would control, exploit, or claim her.

**Emily's Death Made Literal**

The crucial change: Emily dies. Mia shoots her. Emily falls protecting El, and Mia's gun takes her life while El walks unscathed into the sunset. This is not metaphorical; Emily's death is actual and irrevocable. By making Emily's death literal rather than dreamlike, Draft 7 sharpens the moral stakes. El's refusal to engage violently does not protect those around her. The innocent suffer consequences for El's choices.

**Physical Wound Centralized**

The bleeding nostril achieves its full symbolic weight. This is not decorative trauma; this is Julia's signature, her permanent alteration of El's body. The wound persists through El's escape, appears again when she smiles in sunlight ("Her nose wound splits and starts to bleed again"), becomes evidence she cannot hide. Unlike Draft 6, where the wound remains largely internal metaphor, Draft 7 makes the bleeding nostril undeniably, repeatedly visible—a monument to Julia's successful violation.

**Julia Confirms Sheen's Death**

Critically, Julia explicitly states during the drugging sequence: "In most ways, we're glad she's dead." The antecedent is Sheen. Julia confirms the death that occurred at the arrest sequence—Sheen has overdosed, has been removed from the narrative, is now weaponized as absence. This revelation forces El to confront not just her own captivity but her complicity in Sheen's death.

**The "No" Evolution Completes**

Throughout the early narrative, El's refusal is vocalized—"No" mouthed, signed, gestured. At the chateau, during interrogation by the judge (now explicitly Officer Dara, now also Judge Dara), El's "No" becomes purely internal thought. The screenplay indicates "*Nope...*" in stage direction—her refusal is now thought-language rather than spoken or embodied language. When asked if she has questions for Jeannie's testimony, El's response is "*Nope...*"—a quiet, internal negation that requires no external expression.

The evolution is complete: "No" has moved from spoken utterance (Treatment) to mouthed gesture (D4-D6) to internal thought-language (D7). It has become the most private form of refusal, the one thing Julia cannot extract or violate.

**The Trial Sequence**

The trial builds its case on El's refusal to comply with Hugh's demand for help, expanding the charge to include "not respecting elders," "disturbing the peace," "not going to church," "being a good Christian." The prosecution's opening statement explicitly articulates the crime: "the Defendant is a disturber of the peace, a vigilante, and most of all, and most importantly, a little girl who needs to learn her place in this world."

El's defense is her silence coupled with her deck of cards. She hands the jury a card reading "it wasn't me"—a literal deflection of responsibility disguised as linguistic evidence. When asked if she has questions, she shakes her head violently "No!" Judge Dara observes: "This won't play well to our adult demographic." The trial is rigged from its inception. The machinery of law exists to force El's compliance, not to adjudicate justice.

**Final Image**

Draft 7 concludes not with resolution but with sustained ambiguity about whether El's final defiant pleasure—her smile, her speed, her deliberate separation from Hugh—constitutes victory or merely the performance of autonomy in a world that will punish her for it. The screenplay ends in medias res, with the trial still proceeding, El still shaking her head "No," the judicial machinery still grinding toward predetermined judgment.

## Conclusion: The Evolution of Refusal

Across seven major drafts, EL transforms from a coming-of-age mystery about a girl investigating her friend's death into a surrealist examination of bodily autonomy, institutional violence, and the politics of silence. The screenplay's central question—what does it mean for a mute girl to say "no"?—receives increasingly complex answers as the drafts progress.

In the Treatment, El's "no" is vocalized triumph: she speaks for the first time, claiming her voice against parental demand. By D7, the "no" has become pure interiority—a thought that cannot be extracted, a refusal that requires no audience. This evolution suggests the author's deepening understanding that voice-as-speech is not the same as voice-as-selfhood. El's power lies not in speaking but in refusing to participate in the systems that would consume her.

The bleeding nostril, Emily's death, Julia's explicit confirmation of Sheen's overdose—these additions in the final drafts darken the narrative without undermining its surrealist comedy. El survives, but survival is marked, witnessed, permanent. Her smile in sunlight as her nose bleeds again is the screenplay's final image of compromised victory: she has refused, she has escaped, she has said no—and the wound will never fully heal.
