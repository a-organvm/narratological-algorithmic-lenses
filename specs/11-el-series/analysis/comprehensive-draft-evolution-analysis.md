## 2. Draft Synopses (Chronological)

### 2.1 "A Girl and Her Dogs" Era (October-November 2017)

#### Draft D0 (2017-10-31) — ~2,900 lines

**Opening:** Police raid on apartment complex. El (14, mute, adopted) and her dog Albie crouch in bushes watching.

**Act One:** Sandra and Dave discuss financial hardship (Dave lost job). They suggest El get a job; she chooses dog-walking. El experiences school bullying from Mia via text. She meets Hugh (elderly, rambling monologist) on the bus—visualized through absurdist cartoon sequences. El babysits for Sheen (20, artist, drug user), who becomes her mentor figure. They bond over art, cook together, paint nails. El witnesses Sheen's drug use.

**Act Two:** El's dog-walking business grows. Sheen is found dead (ruled overdose). El discovers blood, drugs, polaroids with mysterious faces in Sheen's apartment. She begins amateur investigation, interviewing neighbors with laminated questionnaire cards. Suspects emerge: Gary (drug dealer, apartment worker), Sal (dealer with yellow Corvette), Walter (Jeannie's young husband seen in polaroids).

**Act Three:** El identifies Gary as primary suspect through timeline analysis of parking lot departure. She breaks into his apartment, finds matching drug baggies. Parents confront her about obsessive investigation. Police reveal autopsy: overdose from heroin cut with elephant tranquilizer. El creates Tumblr blog for Sheen's artwork; it goes viral. Miami art contractor offers to display Sheen's work in Wynwood. El declines, prioritizing family over investigation.

**Key Characters:** El (mute 14yo), Sandra/Dave (adoptive parents), Sheen (dead mentor), Albie (dog), Hugh/Barb (elderly neighbors), Gary (suspect), Sal (drug dealer), Officer Billy.

**Tonal Quality:** Mildly absurdist, bittersweet, grounded mystery with cartoon visualization sequences.

---

#### Draft D1.5 (2017-11-28) — ~2,900 lines

**Major Structural Change:** Opens with three conflicting death visualizations:
1. Sheen naked and dead in yellow Corvette trunk, hog-tied
2. Sheen on toilet with sliced wrists ("longways for results")
3. Sheen on bed with foaming drink, shadowy male figure

**New Character:** Julian (scarred Eastern European apartment complex owner). Predatory toward El—touches her inappropriately; she bites his hand and flees. Controls security footage.

**Darkening Elements:**
- Dave shown "licking Sheen's toes" in degrading scene
- Jeannie/Walter revealed as potentially murderous (discussed staging gun-in-mouth suicide)
- College kids scene includes cauldron labeled "fuck bitches" (roofies)
- Fantasy execution sequence: El in military garb executing bullying girls

**Narrative Shift:** From "innocent girl solves mystery" toward "vulnerable child navigates dangerous adults while seeking truth."

---

### 2.2 "Tale of El" Era (December 2017-March 2018)

#### Draft D2 (2017-12-08) — 87 pages

**Title Significance:** Shift to "Tale" suggests folkloric/literary elevation beyond teen drama.

**Opening:** Yellow Corvette trunk containing Sheen's dead body. Flash-cuts of conflicting death scenarios. Police arrive at apartment complex.

**El's Age:** 14, confirmed as "mute Latina" (changed from "Seminole").

**Plot Structure:** Three-act mystery thriller:
- Act One (pp. 1-30): El's world, parents' request, Sheen's death discovery
- Act Two (pp. 31-70): Investigation escalation, Julian/Sal confrontations
- Act Three (pp. 71-87): Family confrontation, email from Miami, declining art offer

**Key Scene Additions:**
- Julian's mansion with surveillance room showing El's apartment
- Sal confrontation at gunpoint in yellow Corvette
- Dave's skeleton key enabling El to enter Gary's apartment
- El speaks for first time reading Miami art email aloud

**Ending:** El declines Miami offer: "Though the offer is a dream come true—Unfortunately, I must decline."

---

#### Drafts D2.5-D3.5 (2017-12-20 through 2018-03-13)

**Primary Changes:** Professional screenplay formatting, minor dialogue refinements, character description clarifications. Core narrative unchanged.

**Notable Refinements:**
- Sandra's line expanded: "Questions a little girl like you should not even be musing about"
- "Connecticut" replaces "Massachusetts" (Sandy Hook reference made specific)
- El's communication via cards explicitly defined as reverse-side playing cards

### 2.4 "El" Era (September 2018-August 2019)

#### Draft D4 (2018-09-26) — 4,614 lines, ~116 scenes

**El's Age:** 14 (consistent with earlier drafts)

**Major Additions:**

**Surrealist Opening:** Vines envelop El; briefcase swallows torso; papers swarm. (Absent from earlier naturalistic openings.)

**Chateau Sequence:** El guided to Julian's fortress featuring:
- Moat with prehistoric alligators
- Nightclub-style interior with strobe lights
- Surveillance room with cameras watching her apartment
- Julian (19) drugs her, moves her to his room
- El escapes by stabbing Alek's eyes, stealing remote control

**Absurdist Courtroom:** Everyone (Judge, Prosecutor, Officer, Jury) named "Daryl." El charged with libel, disturbing crime scenes. Counselor appears as spider-armed creature.

**Ending:** El mouthing "No" to Sandra's demand to give away Albie.

**Key Difference from Treatment:** Julian remains male; "No" mouthed rather than spoken.

---

#### Draft D5 (2019-04-07) — 3,880 lines

**El's Age:** 16 (changed from 14)

**Major Additions:**

**Body Metaphor:** Opens with "The Body" (capitalized dissociative entity) dropping glowing orb. Pharmaceutical/medical framing added.

**Frank's Vehicle:** Red race-car (changed from yellow)

**Counselor Evolution:** Explicit transformation into eight-legged spider-human hybrid during confrontation.

**Julia Character:** Julia (20) replaces Julian. Descends staircase with halo transforming into crown of thorns. Religious imagery significantly expanded.

**Apostles:** Named characters (Uda, Petra) among Julia's followers.

---

#### Draft D6 (2019-04-22) — 3,880 lines, 100+ scenes

**Mia Character Expansion:** Central antagonist in Western gunslinger sequence. Initially hostile to El, eventually receives her compassion.

**Authority Figure Feminization:** Judge/Officer now "Dara" (female) instead of "Daryl" (male).

**Julia Backstory:** Father's disappointment revealed: "I'm dying...I wanted a boy."

**Scene Numbering:** Comprehensive #1# through #100#+ system indicates structural maturation.

---

#### Draft D7 (2019-08-01) — 3,912 lines, 129 scenes

**Final Major Revision**

**Body Metaphor Centralized:** Opens with pharmaceutical dissociation; Body as marked site throughout.

**Emily's Death:** Western gunslinger sequence now ends with Mia shooting Emily (El's defender)—tragic consequences of compassion.

**Physical Wound:** Julia slices El's nostril as punishment. Bleeding persists through final act (visible trauma evidence).

**Counselor Scene:** Now involves both El and Mia simultaneously in therapy confrontation.

**Julia's Monologue Expanded:** "It feels so good to accomplish tenfold what at one-fold was wildly thought of as out of my capabilities."

**Ending Evolution:** "No" now presented as internal thought rather than spoken word. El's final assertion is through smile and firm non-verbal refusal.

**Key Changes from D4:**

| El's Age | 14 | 16 |
| Antagonist | Julian (male, 19) | Julia (female, 20) |
| Vehicle Color | Yellow | Red |

## 3. Feedback Document Extraction

### 3.1 Aaron Avis — First Act Comments (November 8, 2017)

**Draft Addressed:** D0, Act One only

**Criticisms:**
- Cartoon montage needs specific imagery breakdown
- El's silence motivation insufficiently established
- Yellow Corvette and Gary conflated; should be distinct
- Dog-talking gimmick needs variation tied to growth

**Suggestions:**
- Give Dave/Sandra redeeming qualities (show artistic connection attempt)
- Infuse cartoon sequences with El's memories and dark thoughts
- Develop guilt for parents' death (car accident where she was talking)
- Yellow Corvette person should have complicated Sheen history
- Gary as red herring; Yellow Corvette drives by during "murder"
- Explore Sheen's graffiti wall for character revelation
- Vary dog interactions with distinct personalities

**Praise:** Dave/Sandra relationship works; strong thematic potential.

---

### 3.2 Aaron Avis — Second Draft Comments (November 23, 2017)

**Draft Addressed:** D1, full draft

**Criticisms:**
- Sandra/Dave and dog-walking scenes feel repetitive
- Police involvement dismissed too quickly
- Dave's apartment access underutilized
- Background info in descriptions should be minimized

**Suggestions:**
- Add El/Sheen connection scenes (bakery encounter)
- Give dogs distinct personalities as character projection
- Flip hug moment: have Sheen hold longer (informs later bullying scene)
- Develop inept/bored police character introduced before investigation
- Dave should have reflective moment before leaving key
- Exploit Facebook Messenger scene for electronic addiction theme

**Praise:** El nails balance of innocence and quirkiness; Sandra's "I am not her mother" line effective; El/Sheen connection feels earned.

---

### 3.3 Adam — Second Draft Comments (December 18, 2017)

**Draft Addressed:** D2 (marginal annotations, ~21,140 words)

**Key Observations:**
- Opening arm/medical sequence needs major clarity
- Vines metaphor confusing: sometimes literal, sometimes metaphorical
- Action sequences need clearer active voice
- Minor character descriptions flat
- Reality/fantasy transitions need clearer markers (music shifts, visual cues)
- Scene with Frank following El unclear on timing/proximity
- Multiple questions about visual staging and blocking

**Positive Notes:** "I love how you have these little moments of growth for her"; likes college kids comedy section.

---

### 3.4 Rowland — Fourth Draft Comments (October 24, 2018)

**Draft Addressed:** D4 (extensive marginal feedback, 708KB)

**Criticisms:**
- Character introductions confusing (third-person references)
- Inconsistent reality/fantasy boundaries
- Scene descriptions overcomplicated with minute details
- Staging unclear in multiple sequences
- Some dialogue clichéd
- Excessive focus on minor details vs. narrative momentum

**Suggestions:**
- Clarify reality markers earlier and distinctly
- Tighten action sequences
- Consider reducing imaginative sections or using notebook illustration style
- Show El's emotional reactions through action
- Better distinguish perception vs. reality
- More periodic hallucinatory sequences (absent for stretches)
- Clarify courtroom as completely imaginary

**Praise:** Visual imagination and ambition; small growth moments; dog park sequence; diner tension.

### 3.5 Academy Nicholl Fellowships — Reader Comments (2019)

**Draft Addressed:** Nicholl submission (April 2019)

**Reader 1:**

*Praise:* El vibrant despite inability to communicate; real visual sense; vines imagery visually magical; dog-walker concept provides temporary narrative spine; successfully puts audience in moment.

*Criticisms:* Plot-light overall; no stakes for dog-walking; random encounters lack shaping; script loses direction after Sheen dies; increasingly surreal making scenes difficult to interpret; action sequence with bullies/gun unclear; courtroom appears completely imaginary with no clear function; ends without resolving anything.

**Reader 2:**

*Praise:* Voice imaginative and confident; original and told with conviction; magic in visuals.

*Criticisms:* Didn't understand story from first scene; couldn't distinguish real vs. fantasy; no narrative clues for fantasy/fact; fantasy could illuminate muteness but doesn't; ending suggests El found voice but "No" isn't written as dialogue (she only thinks it); repetitive scenes; family dynamic unclear; doesn't understand why El doesn't speak.

*Suggestion:* "Another draft would be worth the effort."

### 5.2 Resistance Patterns: Author's Drift Philosophy

**1. Voice Protection Over Clarity**

The author consistently prioritizes distinctive voice (absurdist visualization, lyrical description, ambiguous fantasy) over reader clarity. Every feedback source mentions confusion; every draft maintains ambiguity.

*Evidence:* Nicholl's "couldn't distinguish real vs. fantasy" met with expanded Body metaphor adding another layer of abstraction.

**2. Ambiguity as Feature, Not Bug**

The "No" evolution is paradigmatic: feedback suggested making it spoken/clearer; author made it more internal. The ending's power derives from what remains unsaid.

*Evidence:* Treatment's explicit "with her voice she says, 'no'" → D7's internal thought. Movement away from directness.

**3. Structural Innovation Over Plot Density**

Plot-light critique appears in Aaron, Rowland, and Nicholl feedback. Author's response: add Body metaphor, elaborate chateau sequence, expand courtroom absurdism—but never add conventional plot mechanics.

*Evidence:* 117 treatment scenes → 116 D4 scenes → 129 D7 scenes. Expansion is psychological/visual, not event-driven.

**4. Gender Transformation as Thematic Deepening**

Julian → Julia transformation (no external feedback catalyst) represents author's deepest independent innovation. Male threat → female manipulation suggests:
- Complicity in patriarchal systems
- Internalized misogyny as structural violence
- Betrayal within supposed solidarity

Daryl → Dara follows same logic: institutional authority feminized to complicate simple gendered reading.

**5. Ending as Voice Integration, Not Voice Expression**

Feedback consistently misreads the "No" as needing spoken delivery. Author's drift suggests the project's meaning: El's voice isn't about speaking—it's about selfhood integration. The Body metaphor, the internal "No," the smile—these constitute voice-as-being rather than voice-as-speech.
